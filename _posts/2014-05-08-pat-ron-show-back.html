Broadcasters have a truly difficult task of delivering play by play and analysis to the layman in layman’s terms. After that, they still need to add insight and understanding to the person who considers himself or herself an "expert."

They serve as our eyes and ears during a baseball game, and the good ones can hit a few more senses.

We need broadcasters, both on radio and TV. And just in case you don’t think so, take this amazing failure of an experiment from 33 years ago.

The problem with needing them is that you sometimes end up with Ken "Hawk" Harrelson, who has a website dedicated to seeing he relinquishes the mic.

And rarely, do you get someone you’d be happy to hear read the dictionary aloud.

[caption id="" align="alignright" width="424"] The "Pat and Ron Show" is back in the Wrigley Field press box. (Photo by Flickr user D.L.)[/caption]

So, mostly you’re stuck with someone in the middle, which begs a simple question: What do you want in a broadcaster?

For me, loving a broadcaster boils down to likability with a potential for memorability wrapped inside a general knowledge of the game. A broadcaster must be interesting — not self-interested.

What we’re talking about here is the dirty little secret behind all platforms of sports media. While the news section demands objectivity (or so people think), sports writing and sports broadcasting is a performance.

And like the best actors, the best sports people build a bond with the audience because they speak to them not at them. Cockiness is annoying.

We loved Harry Caray because he was a fan of the game, said names backwards and knew how to play the naive role. He was a showman and one with no pretension.

We loved Ron Santo because he, too, was a fan, and he was genuine — and perhaps more genuinely confused than Harry played.

All of this build-up is to simply say that Coomer is a nice addition to the Cubs radio booth.

Coomer did very little to stand out as a Chicago Cubs utility player in 2001 — or his entire career, for that matter — so it makes me smile when Pat Hughes introduces him as "former all-star Ron Coomer" like he used to intro Ron Santo as a "nine-time all star and five-time Gold Glove winner."

But what Coomer lacks in accolades from his playing days he makes up for in realness. Fans want a person they feel like they can identify with.

Keith Moreland simply didn’t have that, which proves to me that experience behind the mic doesn’t matter much. "Zonk" spent 16 years calling college baseball before he joined the Cubs in 2011 only to leave after two seasons. The official word was that he left to spend more time with family. And who am I to suspect otherwise? But… you know…

Moreland knew the game, indeed. But he didn’t fit well next to Hughes. Pat plays the straight man and his sidekick the cutup.

But when the sidekick forgets who really runs the show, it gets awkward.

Coomer brings a perfect mixture of knowledge, affability and simplicity. He understands his role and plays it well. His somewhat unrefined half-laughing delivery and nervous chuckles will sharpen as the two men in the booth learn each other's timing.

The "Pat and Ron Show" is back and with a Ron as equally jolly as the last. Simply to hear Pat say, "Pat and Ron," is nostalgic and familiar. I like it.

Now if we can only loosen up J.D. a little bit.
