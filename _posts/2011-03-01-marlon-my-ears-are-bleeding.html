Sometimes Byrd seems to swoop in out of nowhere to dive for a ball.  Sometimes Byrd flies around the bases, trying for an extra-base hit.  Or sometimes Byrd even leads his team south to warmer climes before a triumphant return to the north side in April.  But sometimes a Byrd takes a crap on something you love.


This past Saturday, Paul Sullivan wrote a piece about the Cubs considering getting rid of the new plate-approach music at Wrigley Field that was implemented in the middle of the 2010 season.  Apparently, a fan at the Cubs Convention asked Tom Ricketts "why they were spoiling 'the Wrigley Field experience'" by replacing the organ music with excerpts of pop music, played while players are walking to the plate.  In the article, Marlon Byrd admits that he is the player who originally asked Tom Ricketts for a switch from organ music to "snippets of taped music for hitters' intro songs."  Marlon, what were you thinking?  Well, it does sound like his intentions were good.  Byrd seemed to think that it would boost team morale, but we all know how that turned out.  Anyhow, this situation got me wondering about the history of the organ at Wrigley.

According to BallparkTour.com, the Cubs "brought an organ into Wrigley Field as a one-day only gimmick" in 1941.  The fans enjoyed it so much, that they made the organ a permanent fixture at the park, and it began to catch on with other teams as well.  For some reason, organ music just seems to meld well with a baseball game on a summer's day; it is music that colors the casual atmosphere and matches the pacing of the sport.  It's a beautiful thing.
