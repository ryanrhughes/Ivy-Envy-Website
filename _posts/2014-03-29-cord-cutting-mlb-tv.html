We talked about our Cubs new year’s resolutions on Podcast Episode5.41. Mine was to drop the $85/month TV subscription (the minimum on Dish network to get WGN and Comcast SportsNet) and cut the cord—go old school and get to know Pat and CoomDog on WGN radio really well. The only thing keeping me (and many others) is live sports, particularly Cubs baseball. I can manage without the rest of the garbage on the 200+ channels.

Most cord-cutting baseball fans have MLB.tv to turn to. I live in Iowa. I am considered “local” market for the Cubs (223 miles), White Sox (223 miles), Cardinals (261 miles), Brewers (265 miles), Twins (303 miles) and Royals (307 miles). If all those cities were “local”, Iowa City would be the greatest place in the world to live. All games in which any of those teams play would be blacked out for me. There are ways around it, but I won’t detail those here. Google it. If you’re web savvy you can make it happen for a few extra bucks a month.

This past week, I finally pulled the trigger. Even with the massively frustrating blackout policy, MLB.tv is a pretty good deal at $129.99 a year (postseason not included). I added MiLB.tv for another $25 to get my Baez fix. 90 minutes after games conclude they are available as an archive without blackout. There is also a condensed game feature allowing you to watch all the important plays of a game in rapid fire without commentary. I imagine the condensed games will be a regular part of my evenings when the Cubs play during work hours.

I’ve watched about 4 games on MLB.TV. This is what I have learned so far:

• Customer service is non-existent. Spend 2 hours trying to figure it out on your own. Then call.

• It takes some amount of technical knowledge to navigate issues. Don’t encourage your grandparents to sign up if they can’t install plugins, clear the cache, or navigate buffering issues.

• The iPad version of the app is amazing with live stat overlays and DVR features. The iPhone version is good, but not as good as the iPad.

• Video quality is very good with occasional jitters. If you have reliable download speeds (mine are 12-20 mbps) you won't have many issues streaming the HD video.

• You don't get to see the Chicago commercials. Odd to say, but a good old 866-4-FELDCO or Bob Rohrman in your face has become part of the experience of watching a game on TV.

The blackout policy doesn't apply during Spring Training. We’re rapidly approaching opening day so I’ll keep you posted on how it’s going. If it goes well you probably won’t hear a lot about it. If not, there may be an angrier version of Kurt on future podcasts.
