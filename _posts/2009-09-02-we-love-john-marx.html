and he loves the Cubs.  In one of Marx's recent articles for The Dispatch he writes, "In the past 30 days, the Cubs have slipped in the standings, and are now in danger of having the season's towel tossed in.  Now wouldn't that make for a perfect ending to a near-perfect summer?"  So, what you're telling me, sir, is that you like reveling in other team's misfortunes more than celebrating your own favorite team's successes?  And what team does Mr. Marx like that is helping him to have this "near-perfect summer?"  He never mentions that team's name in his article.  Instead, the focus is on how much of a "Cub loather" he is.  He goes on to inform readers of his future "whereabouts when the Cubs win the World Series" which will be "the cemetery plot [he has] purchased."  God, please help the Cubs pull off a miracle and win the series this year.  I don't want to "wait 'til next year" anymore. 

 

We've already made known our feelings toward fans who expend more energy hating a team than they do cheering on their own, so I want to talk about something else.  Studies show that the areas of the brain activated by hate are the same areas activated by love.  There is a fine line between love and hate, so I choose to celebrate my indifference toward the Cardinals.  So, even though right now Marx is spewing acrimonious words at the Cubs and Cubs fans, I wouldn't be surprised if his relationship with the Cubs ends up like the formula to so many romantic comedies--it begins with complete disdain and ends with the pair clinking wine glasses together in a tropical locale.
