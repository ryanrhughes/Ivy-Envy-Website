This news is a couple days old, but the Tennessee Smokies have been named "organization of the year" in Minor League Baseball by the National Organization of Professional Baseball Leagues.  The award is the John H. Johnson President’s Award (first awarded in 1974), and it is awarded annually to the team determined to be the most stable minor league franchise.  The winning team also must contribute significantly to its community, league, and the baseball industry in general.  Winning this award is a great honor for the Smokies, who are the first Southern League team to receive it.  This year, the Smokies averaged 3,960 fans per game, which is the highest they have ever had.  The team won the North Division of the Southern League for the third year in a row and just missed winning it all.

Given the emphasis the Cubs are going to placing on their farm system going forward, this is a significant accolade.  Many people have been dragging the Cubs' minor league system through the mud lately.  It has been in our consciousness due to compensation negotiations for Epstein and Hoyer.  The new front office has made it clear that the minor league affiliates will be a big focus for the Cubs going forward, and a significant amount of resources and money will be used to strengthen the infrastructure of the organization as a whole.  The fact that the Smokies and the other affiliates will be getting better players and won't be gutted all the time for ridiculous trades is exciting.  An already strong organization like the Smokies will just be getting better, and it's nice to see that the situation at the minor league level isn't completely bleak.  Epstein & Co. (threw that in there to anger Kurt) will have some foundation on which to build.
